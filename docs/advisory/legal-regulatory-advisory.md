## 1. Business Registration & Licensing in Iraq

### 1.1 Available Business Entities

| Entity Type | Arabic Term | Key Characteristics | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Limited Liability Company (LLC)** | Sharika Mahdoudat al-Mas'uliyya | 1-25 shareholders, limited liability, minimum capital IQD 1M (~$680 USD) | VisionNetworking's primary recommendation |
| **Joint Stock Company (JSC)** | Sharika Musahama | 5+ founders, can issue shares publicly, minimum capital IQD 2M | Larger-scale operations or future IPO |
| **Sole Proprietorship** | Mu'assasa Fardiyya | Single owner, unlimited liability | Not recommended for tech platforms |
| **General Partnership** | Sharika Tadamun | 2+ partners, joint & several liability | Not recommended |
| **Limited Partnership** | Sharika Tawsiya Basita | Mix of general and limited partners | Rarely used for tech |
| **Branch Office** | Far' Sharika Ajnabiyya | Extension of foreign parent company | If VN is incorporated abroad |

**Recommendation for VisionNetworking:** Register as an **Iraqi LLC**. This provides liability protection, is the most common structure for tech companies, and allows foreign ownership (subject to restrictions below).

### 1.2 Registration Process: Federal Iraq vs. Kurdistan Region (KRI)

#### Federal Iraq (Baghdad and southern/central governorates)

1. **Reserve company name** at the Companies Registrar (Ministry of Trade) -- takes 1-3 days
2. **Draft Articles of Association** -- must be notarized; Arabic is mandatory
3. **Deposit minimum capital** (IQD 1M for LLC) into an Iraqi bank account
4. **Submit registration application** to the Companies Registrar with:
   - Articles of Association (notarized)
   - Bank deposit certificate
   - ID documents of all founders (passports for foreigners)
   - Lease agreement for registered office address
5. **Obtain Commercial Registration Certificate** -- typically 2-6 weeks
6. **Register with the General Commission for Taxes** (tax card)
7. **Register with the Ministry of Labor and Social Affairs** (for employee social security)
8. **Obtain sector-specific licenses** (see 1.3 below)

**Estimated timeline:** 4-12 weeks  
**Estimated cost:** $2,000-$5,000 USD (including legal fees, notarization, registration fees)

#### Kurdistan Region of Iraq (KRI -- Erbil, Sulaymaniyah, Duhok)

The KRI has its own investment board and more streamlined process:

1. **Apply through the Kurdistan Board of Investment (BOI)** if seeking investment incentives (tax holidays, land grants)
2. **Register with the KRI Companies Registrar** (Ministry of Trade, KRI) -- process mirrors federal but is typically faster
3. **Obtain KRI-specific tax registration**
4. **Additional benefit:** KRI Investment Law No. 4 of 2006 offers foreign investors equal treatment to domestic investors, plus potential 10-year tax exemptions for qualifying projects

**Estimated timeline:** 3-8 weeks  
**Estimated cost:** $1,500-$4,000 USD

**Key difference:** KRI is generally more business-friendly, faster to register, and offers better foreign investment incentives. However, if VisionNetworking operates across all of Iraq (38 buildings), it needs federal registration or dual registration.

**Practical note:** Many companies register federally in Baghdad and then obtain a KRI branch license, or vice versa. VN should register where its headquarters will be located and obtain branch authorizations for other regions.

### 1.3 Required Licenses for a Tech/E-Commerce Platform

| License | Issuing Authority | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Commercial Registration** | Ministry of Trade (Companies Registrar) | Core business license |
| **Tax Registration (Tax Card)** | General Commission for Taxes | Tax compliance |
| **Telecommunications/IT License** | Communications and Media Commission (CMC) | If operating telecoms infrastructure or data services |
| **E-Commerce Activity License** | Ministry of Trade | Specific authorization for online commercial activity |
| **Industry-Specific Approvals** | Varies | Construction-related activities may require Ministry of Construction & Housing notification |

**Important:** Iraq does not yet have a comprehensive tech licensing framework. The CMC oversees telecom and internet services, but a pure software platform like VisionNetworking typically falls under general commercial registration. Monitor this -- Iraq is actively developing digital economy regulations.

### 1.4 Foreign Ownership Rules

**Federal Iraq (Companies Law No. 21 of 1997, as amended):**
- Foreign investors can own up to **100% of an Iraqi LLC** in most sectors
- **Exceptions:** Foreign ownership is restricted or prohibited in:
  - Real estate (foreigners cannot own land; leasing is permitted)
  - Natural resources extraction
  - Banking and insurance (caps apply)
  - Media
- Foreign-owned companies must have at least one **Iraqi national as a local agent or representative**
- VisionNetworking as a tech platform faces no foreign ownership restrictions

**KRI (Investment Law No. 4 of 2006):**
- Foreign investors treated equally to domestic investors
- 100% foreign ownership permitted in most sectors
- Additional incentives: duty-free imports of equipment, tax holidays

**National Investment Law No. 13 of 2006:**
- Provides federal-level investment incentives
- Foreign investors can lease land for up to 50 years
- Projects registered with the National Investment Commission (NIC) may receive tax exemptions

### 1.5 Tax Obligations

#### Corporate Income Tax
- **Standard rate:** 15% on taxable income (under Income Tax Law No. 113 of 1982, as amended)
- **Applies to:** All companies operating in Iraq, including foreign-owned
- **Filing:** Annual tax return due within 5 months of fiscal year-end
- **Advance payments:** Required quarterly based on prior year's tax

#### Withholding Tax
- **Payments to non-residents:** 15% withholding on services, management fees, royalties
- **Employee salaries:** PAYE (Pay As You Earn) withholding by employer
- **Contractor payments:** 3.3% retention tax on government contracts; similar applied to private sector in practice

#### Value Added Tax (VAT)
- **Iraq does not currently impose VAT** (as of early 2026)
- There is a **sales tax** of 300% on certain goods (luxury items, alcohol, tobacco) but this does not apply to tech services
- **Monitor closely:** Iraq has been considering VAT introduction as part of fiscal reform; this could affect VisionNetworking's pricing model

#### Social Security Contributions
- **Employer contribution:** 12% of employee gross salary to the Pension and Social Security Fund
- **Employee contribution:** 5% of gross salary (withheld by employer)

#### Stamp Duty
- 0.2% on certain contracts and commercial documents
- Relevant to BOQ-related contracts facilitated through the platform

**Tax Advisory Note:** VisionNetworking should engage an Iraqi-licensed tax accountant from day one. Tax enforcement in Iraq is inconsistent, but non-compliance creates serious risk, especially as Iraq modernizes its tax administration.

## 2. Data Privacy & Protection

### 2.1 Current Data Protection Laws in Iraq

**Iraq does not have a comprehensive, standalone data protection law** comparable to the EU's GDPR or similar frameworks. Data protection is addressed through a patchwork of provisions:

- **Iraqi Constitution (2005), Article 17(2):** Guarantees the right to personal privacy and prohibits monitoring of communications except with judicial authorization
- **Iraqi Penal Code (Law No. 111 of 1969):** Criminalizes unauthorized disclosure of personal secrets and private communications
- **Informatics Crimes Law (Cybercrime Law, enacted 2020-2023 window):** Iraq has been working on cybercrime legislation; enforcement and scope remain evolving. This law addresses unauthorized access, data theft, and electronic fraud
- **Telecommunications Law:** The CMC has regulatory authority over data transmitted via telecom networks
- **KRI:** The Kurdistan Region follows federal Iraqi law on these matters but has shown interest in adopting more progressive digital governance frameworks

**Practical implication:** The absence of a dedicated data protection law does not mean VisionNetworking can be careless with data. Iraqi courts can and do adjudicate privacy violations under the Constitution and Penal Code. Additionally, if VN handles data of EU nationals or processes data through EU-based servers, GDPR may apply extraterritorially.

### 2.2 Personal Data VisionNetworking Collects

Based on VN's platform operations, the following personal data categories are likely collected:

| Data Category | Examples | Sensitivity Level |
|---|---|---|
| **Identity Data** | Full names, company names, job titles | Medium |
| **Contact Data** | Phone numbers, email addresses, physical addresses | Medium |
| **Financial Data** | Bank details, payment information, pricing | High |
| **BOQ/Project Data** | Bills of quantities, material specifications, project scopes | High (commercially sensitive) |
| **Usage Data** | Login history, platform activity, search queries | Low-Medium |
| **Communication Data** | Messages between clients and suppliers via platform | Medium |
| **Location Data** | Building addresses, delivery locations across 38 sites | Medium |
| **Device/Technical Data** | IP addresses, browser type, device identifiers | Low |

### 2.3 Data Residency Requirements

- Iraq **does not currently mandate** data localization (i.e., there is no law requiring data to be stored on servers physically located in Iraq)
- However, the CMC has regulatory authority over data transmitted through Iraqi telecom infrastructure
- **Practical recommendation:** Host primary data within Iraq or the broader Middle East region (e.g., AWS Bahrain, Azure UAE) for performance and to demonstrate good faith compliance with emerging regulations
- **Government contracts:** If VN serves government entities, there may be contractual requirements to store project data within Iraq

### 2.4 Compliance Obligations

Even without a comprehensive data protection law, VisionNetworking should implement:

1. **Consent mechanisms** -- Obtain clear consent before collecting personal data, particularly for marketing communications
2. **Data security measures** -- Encryption at rest and in transit, access controls, regular security audits
3. **Data breach notification process** -- While not legally mandated, establish internal procedures to detect, investigate, and respond to data breaches
4. **Third-party data processing agreements** -- If sharing data with suppliers, payment processors, or cloud providers, formalize data handling obligations
5. **Employee data handling** -- Iraqi labor law creates implicit obligations to protect employee records

### 2.5 Privacy Policy Recommendations

VisionNetworking should publish a bilingual (Arabic/English) privacy policy covering:

- **What data is collected** and why (see table above)
- **Legal basis for processing** -- consent, contractual necessity, legitimate interest
- **How data is used** -- matching clients with suppliers, generating quotations, improving AI algorithms
- **Data sharing** -- who receives data (suppliers, payment processors, analytics providers)
- **Data retention periods** -- how long data is kept and when it is deleted
- **User rights** -- access, correction, deletion of personal data (adopt GDPR-like rights as best practice even though not legally required)
- **Cookies and tracking** -- if the platform uses cookies or analytics tools
- **Children's data** -- confirm the platform is not directed at minors
- **Cross-border data transfers** -- disclose if data leaves Iraq
- **Contact information** for data-related inquiries
- **Updates policy** -- how users are notified of privacy policy changes

**Cost estimate for privacy policy drafting:** $1,500-$3,000 USD (Iraqi law firm) or $3,000-$7,000 USD (international firm with Iraqi practice)

## 3. E-Commerce & Digital Business Regulations

### 3.1 E-Commerce Laws in Iraq

**Iraq does not have a dedicated e-commerce law.** Key points:

- The **Iraqi Civil Code (Law No. 40 of 1951)** governs contracts generally and can apply to online transactions, but it was not written with digital commerce in mind
- The **Electronic Signature and Electronic Transactions Law** (draft legislation has been under discussion for several years; check current status with Iraqi counsel) would, once enacted, provide a legal framework for e-commerce
- **KRI:** Has shown more progressive interest in digital economy legislation but similarly lacks a comprehensive e-commerce statute
- **In practice:** Online businesses operate under general commercial law, and disputes are resolved through the civil courts

### 3.2 Electronic Signature Validity

- Iraq's legal framework **does not yet fully recognize electronic signatures** as equivalent to handwritten signatures for all purposes
- The draft Electronic Signature and Electronic Transactions Law would change this if enacted
- **Current practice:** Important contracts (especially those involving real estate, government entities, or large sums) should still be executed with wet ink signatures
- **Platform implication for VN:** Quotations and BOQs generated through the platform should include disclaimers that they constitute preliminary proposals and that binding contracts require formal execution per applicable law
- **Workaround:** Use electronic acceptance mechanisms (click-to-accept) for platform terms of service, but advise users that construction contracts may require physical signatures for enforceability

### 3.3 Online Contract Enforceability

- **General rule:** Iraqi courts will enforce contracts formed online if the essential elements of contract formation under the Civil Code are met:
  1. Offer and acceptance (can be electronic)
  2. Capacity of parties
  3. Lawful object
  4. Consideration
- **Evidence:** Electronic records (emails, platform logs, chat histories) can be submitted as evidence, though judges have discretion on their evidentiary weight
- **Recommendation:** VN's platform should maintain detailed audit logs of all transactions, quotations, acceptances, and communications. These serve as critical evidence in any dispute

### 3.4 Consumer Protection

- **Consumer Protection Law No. 1 of 2010** provides basic consumer protections:
  - Right to accurate product information
  - Prohibition on deceptive advertising
  - Right to return defective goods
  - Prohibition on monopolistic practices
- **B2B application:** While primarily B2C-focused, these principles extend to commercial transactions, and courts may apply them to protect smaller businesses dealing with larger suppliers
- **VN's role:** As a platform connecting parties, VN should clearly define its liability limitations -- is VN a marketplace (connecting parties) or a principal (making commitments itself)?
- **Recommendation:** Draft Terms of Service that clearly position VN as a marketplace/intermediary, not a guarantor of supplier performance or product quality

### 3.5 Payment Processing Regulations

- **Central Bank of Iraq (CBI)** regulates all payment and banking activities
- **Electronic payment services** require CBI licensing or partnership with a CBI-licensed entity
- **Mobile money** is growing (e.g., Qi Card, ZainCash, FastPay, NassPay) -- these are CBI-licensed
- **Foreign payment processors** (Stripe, PayPal) do not officially operate in Iraq; VN cannot rely on them
- **Practical options for VN:**
  1. Partner with an Iraqi CBI-licensed payment processor (Qi Card, ZainCash)
  2. Use bank transfers through Iraqi commercial banks
  3. Implement cash-on-delivery for physical goods (common in Iraqi e-commerce)
  4. Consider escrow-like arrangements for large BOQ transactions (requires careful legal structuring)
- **Anti-money laundering (AML):** CBI enforces AML regulations; VN must implement Know Your Customer (KYC) procedures for all platform participants
- **Currency:** All domestic transactions should be denominated in Iraqi Dinars (IQD); foreign currency transactions require CBI compliance

## 4. Employment Law Considerations

### 4.1 Iraqi Labor Law Basics

The primary legislation is the **Labor Law No. 37 of 2015**, which replaced the older 1987 law and aligns more closely with ILO conventions.

- Applies to all employees in the private sector
- Does **not** apply to government employees (governed by civil service law), domestic workers, or family businesses with fewer than 5 employees
- **Minimum wage:** Set by the Council of Ministers; as of recent adjustments, approximately IQD 350,000-400,000/month (~$240-$270 USD). Confirm current rate
- **Probation period:** Maximum 3 months; can be terminated by either party during probation with notice

### 4.2 Employment Contracts: Required Terms

All employment relationships must be documented. Contracts should include:

- Full names and addresses of employer and employee
- Job title and description of duties
- Date of commencement
- Contract duration (fixed-term or indefinite)
- Salary and payment frequency (must be at least monthly)
- Working hours
- Leave entitlements
- Probation period (if applicable)
- Termination provisions
- Place of work
- Any special terms or allowances

**Language:** Contracts should be in Arabic. Bilingual (Arabic/English) contracts are acceptable, but Arabic prevails in any dispute.

**Registration:** Employers must register employment contracts with the Ministry of Labor and Social Affairs.

### 4.3 Working Hours, Leave, and Benefits

| Category | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Standard working hours** | 8 hours/day, 48 hours/week |
| **Overtime** | Permitted with employee consent; paid at 150% of regular rate (200% for holidays/rest days) |
| **Weekly rest** | Minimum 1 day per week (typically Friday) |
| **Annual leave** | 20 working days/year (30 days after 5 years of service) |
| **Sick leave** | 30 days at full pay, then 30 days at half pay per year (with medical certificate) |
| **Maternity leave** | 72 days (pre- and post-natal combined), fully paid |
| **Paternity leave** | 3 days |
| **Public holidays** | Approximately 10-14 days/year (varies; includes Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, Newroz, etc.) |
| **End-of-service gratuity** | Not explicitly mandated under the 2015 law (covered by social security contributions), but common practice to provide |

### 4.4 Termination Rules

**Employer-initiated termination:**
- Must be for a **valid reason** (poor performance, misconduct, redundancy, business closure)
- **Notice period:** Minimum 30 days for contracts of indefinite duration
- **Severance pay:** The social security system covers end-of-service benefits. Employers are not separately required to pay severance, but contractual obligations may exist
- **Unfair dismissal:** Employees can challenge termination before the Labor Court. If the court finds the termination unfair, the employer may be ordered to pay compensation (not reinstatement)

**Employee-initiated resignation:**
- 30 days' notice for indefinite contracts
- Fixed-term contracts: employee generally cannot resign before term unless by agreement or for cause

**Summary dismissal (no notice):** Permitted for gross misconduct (e.g., theft, assault, repeated absence without justification, disclosure of trade secrets)

### 4.5 Foreign Worker Hiring Rules

- **Work permits** are required for all foreign employees -- issued by the Ministry of Labor
- **Residency permits** are required from the Ministry of Interior (General Directorate of Residency)
- **Quota system:** Iraqi law prioritizes Iraqi nationals; employers must demonstrate that no qualified Iraqi candidate is available for the position
- **Typical process:**
  1. Obtain a job offer letter from the Iraqi employer
  2. Employer applies for work permit at Ministry of Labor
  3. Employee applies for entry visa at Iraqi embassy
  4. Upon arrival, apply for residency permit
- **KRI:** Slightly different process through KRI's Ministry of Labor, generally considered more efficient
- **Timeline:** 4-8 weeks for work permit; 2-4 weeks for residency
- **Cost:** $500-$2,000 USD per employee (work permit fees, residency fees, legal assistance)

**Practical note for VN:** If hiring developers or AI engineers from outside Iraq, the work permit process can be slow. Consider remote employment through an Employer of Record (EOR) service for non-Iraq-based team members, with a small local team holding work permits.

## 5. Intellectual Property

### 5.1 Trademark Registration in Iraq

**Governing law:** Trademarks and Trade Descriptions Law No. 21 of 1957 (as amended)

**Registration process:**
1. **Conduct a trademark search** through the Iraqi Trademark Office (Ministry of Industry) -- takes 1-2 weeks
2. **File application** with:
   - Mark representation (logo, wordmark)
   - List of goods/services (Nice Classification)
   - Applicant details
   - Power of attorney (notarized and legalized if filed through an agent)
3. **Examination** by the Trademark Office -- 3-6 months
4. **Publication** in the Iraqi Official Gazette for opposition (30 days)
5. **Registration certificate** issued if no opposition
6. **Duration:** 10 years, renewable indefinitely

**Total timeline:** 6-18 months  
**Cost:** $1,000-$2,500 USD per class (including agent fees)

**KRI note:** Trademarks registered federally are generally respected in KRI, but filing separately with KRI authorities provides additional protection.

**Recommendation for VN:** File for "VisionNetworking" (wordmark) and any logo marks in Classes 35 (business services, procurement), 38 (telecommunications), and 42 (software, technology services).

### 5.2 Software/AI Patent Considerations

- **Iraqi Patent Law (Law No. 65 of 1970, as amended):** Patents are available for inventions that are novel, involve an inventive step, and are industrially applicable
- **Software patents:** Iraq, like many jurisdictions, does **not** explicitly grant patents for software "as such." However, software tied to a technical process or hardware may be patentable
- **AI algorithms:** Unlikely to be patentable in Iraq under current law. The AI-powered procurement matching, BOQ analysis, and quotation generation algorithms are better protected through trade secret and copyright
- **Practical approach:** Do not rely on patents for VN's core technology. Instead:
  - Protect source code through copyright (automatic under Iraqi Copyright Law No. 3 of 1971)
  - Protect algorithms and training data as trade secrets
  - Use contractual protections (NDAs, employment IP clauses)

### 5.3 Trade Secret Protection

- **No standalone trade secret law in Iraq**, but protection is available through:
  - **Civil Code:** Claims for unfair competition and breach of confidence
  - **Penal Code:** Criminal liability for disclosure of business secrets
  - **Employment contracts:** Enforceable non-disclosure and non-compete clauses
- **What VN should protect as trade secrets:**
  - AI/ML model architecture and training data
  - Supplier pricing data and algorithms
  - Client purchasing patterns and analytics
  - BOQ analysis methodologies
  - Platform matching algorithms

**Practical steps:**
1. Implement internal classification of confidential information (public, internal, confidential, restricted)
2. Require all employees and contractors to sign NDAs with non-compete clauses (reasonable duration: 1-2 years)
3. Limit access to sensitive systems on a need-to-know basis
4. Maintain access logs and audit trails
5. Include IP assignment clauses in all employment and contractor agreements

## 6. Industry-Specific Regulations

### 6.1 Construction Industry Regulations

VisionNetworking serves the construction sector. Key regulatory bodies and requirements:

- **Ministry of Construction, Housing, Municipalities, and Public Works** -- oversees construction activities, issues contractor classifications
- **Contractor classification system:** Iraqi contractors are classified into grades (special, first, second, etc.) based on capacity, experience, and capital. VN should verify contractor classifications when onboarding suppliers
- **Building codes:** Iraq uses a mix of Iraqi national codes and references to international standards (ACI, ASTM, BS). VN's BOQ data should reference applicable standards
- **Engineering practice:** Engineers must be registered with the **Iraqi Engineers Union** to practice. VN should verify this for any engineering service providers on the platform
- **Safety regulations:** Workplace safety is governed by the Labor Law and specific Ministry of Health and Environment regulations

**Implication for VN:** While VN is a platform and not a contractor, it should:
- Verify contractor licensing/classification before listing them
- Include disclaimers that VN does not guarantee contractor qualifications
- Consider adding a verification badge system for registered/classified contractors

### 6.2 Import/Export Regulations for Building Materials

- **Customs Authority (General Commission for Customs):** All imports must comply with customs regulations and tariff schedules
- **Import licenses:** Required for certain categories of goods; building materials generally do not require special import licenses, but specific materials (e.g., reinforcing steel, cement) may face temporary import controls or duties
- **Standards compliance:** Imported materials must comply with **Iraqi Standard Specifications** issued by the **Central Organization for Standardization and Quality Control (COSQC)**
- **Country-of-origin restrictions:** Iraq maintains trade restrictions with certain countries; verify current sanctions/embargo lists
- **Free zones:** Iraq operates free trade zones (e.g., Basra, Khor al-Zubair) that offer duty exemptions -- relevant if VN facilitates material imports

**Implication for VN:** If the platform facilitates material procurement involving imports:
- Include supplier origin data in the platform
- Flag materials that may face import restrictions
- Provide links to relevant COSQC standards
- Consider integrating customs duty estimation into quotation generation

### 6.3 Quality Standards and Certifications

| Standard/Certification | Relevance |
|---|---|
| **Iraqi Standard Specifications (IQS)** | Mandatory for materials used in Iraqi construction |
| **ISO 9001** | Quality management -- recommended for suppliers on the platform |
| **ISO 14001** | Environmental management -- increasingly relevant for government projects |
| **ASTM/ACI/BS standards** | Referenced in Iraqi building codes for specific materials |
| **COSQC product certifications** | Required for certain construction materials sold in Iraq |

**Recommendation:** VN should build standards compliance indicators into its supplier profiles, allowing clients to filter suppliers by quality certifications.

### 6.4 Government Procurement Rules

If VisionNetworking wants to serve government projects:

- **Government Procurement Law (implementing regulations under the Federal Budget Law and Ministry of Finance directives):**
  - Government contracts above certain thresholds require public tender
  - Preferential treatment for Iraqi companies and products (local content requirements)
  - Government contracts are subject to retention taxes and payment delays (60-120 days is common)
- **Registration with government entities:** VN would need to register as an approved vendor/service provider with each contracting government ministry
- **Board of Supreme Audit:** Government contracts are subject to audit; documentation must be meticulous
- **Corruption risk:** Iraq ranks poorly on corruption indices (Transparency International). VN must implement robust anti-bribery and anti-corruption policies, especially when engaging with government procurement
- **International donor-funded projects:** Projects funded by World Bank, UNDP, USAID, etc. follow their own procurement rules (which are typically more transparent and standardized). VN could target these as lower-risk entry points into government-adjacent work

**Recommendation:** Start with private-sector clients. If pursuing government contracts, do so selectively, with strong compliance infrastructure and legal counsel experienced in Iraqi public procurement.
